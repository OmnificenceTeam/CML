GUIDELINES EXCERPTS

This section contains highlights of the NCCN and ELN guidelines pertaining to the use of TKIs and monitoring response to treatment.

ELN Guidelines 2013
NCCN Guidelines 2014

INTRODUCTION

Progress in CML treatment is so rapid that any recommendation quickly becomes obsolete

Several sources provide information and data

Missions and interests vary from independent investigators to pharma companies

Hence, guidelines should be based on evidence and weighted evidence mainly using prospective randomized studies with a long-term follow-up and only secondarily on phase 2 studies, on preliminary unconfirmed data and on the personal opinions of the experts

DEFINITION AND RECOGNITION OF PHASE OF CML

3 phases

  • Chronic phase (CP)
  • Accelerated phase (AP)
  • Blastic phase (BP)

AP – 15 to 29% blast cells or 30 to 49% blast cells + promyelocytes in blood or marrow or a platelet count <100×109/L unrelated to treatment or a clonal chromosome abnormality in Ph+ cells (CCA/Ph+)

BP – blast cells percentage ≥30% in blood or marrow or by blast cells involvement of non-hematopoietic tissues, excluding liver and spleen

A small but not irrelevant proportion of CML patients present in AP or BP at the time of diagnosis. Majority of patients undergo acceleration and progression to BP after failure of TKI therapy.

Patients presenting in AP respond well to 2ndgeneration TKIs. They are eligible for allogenic SCT in the absence of optimal response to therapy.

Patients presenting in BP also respond well to TKI therapy; however, they are more prone for relapses. Hence, all of them should be considered eligible for SCT.

Patients who progress from CP to AP and BP are less sensitive to any kind of subsequent therapy and hence should receive TKI therapy. All these patients are eligible for SCT.

There are three risk scores, Sokal, EURO, and EUTOS.

All the three scores have been validated.

Patients with a high-risk score or with CCA/Ph+ can probably benefit of a specific risk-adapted therapy, e.g., with a more potent second-generation TKI, but there are no data, no studies, showing that a specific treatment would be of benefit, and if so, how much. Therefore, ELN includes these factors in the warning category, warning that these patients should be monitored more carefully, suggesting that they are eligible for investigational therapies.

ASSESSING AND MONITORING THE RESPONSE

ELN expert panel recommends use of both conventional cytogenetics and real-time quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-Q-PCR)

Cytogenetics

  • Conventional cytogenetics by chromosome banding analysis (CBA) of at least 20 marrow cells metaphases performed at 3, 6, and 12 months until CCyR is achieved
  • CBA can be substituted by fluorescence-in-situ-hybridization (FISH) only after CCyR is achieved

RT-Q-PCR

  • RT-Q-PCR is more sensitive and does not require marrow sampling
  • If RT-Q-PCR is available with the standardized methodology and expressed as BCR-ABL1% IS, it should be used from the beginning of treatment
  • RT-Q-PCR can be performed once every 3-6 months after MMR is achieved depending on baseline risk, transcripts level, and transcripts level fluctuations

Assay sensitivity should be defined in a standardized manner when BCR-ABL1 mRNA is undetectable

The phrase “complete molecular response” should not be used; “molecularly undetectable leukemia,” with specification of the number of control gene transcript copies, is preferred

Molecular monitoring is recommended monthly for 1 year and at least every 3 months thereafter in patients who have discontinued therapy after achieving stable or deep molecular response

Molecular + Cytogenetic testing is recommended in patients whose response is borderline or fluctuating. Cytogenetic testing is necessary in CCA/Ph- patients and patients with atypical BCR-ABL1 transcripts.

Mutational analysis is recommended in AP and BP, both prior to therapy as well as during therapy

Mutational analysis in CP is mandatory in case of failure; however it is recommended in case of warning

There are three grades of response that are clinically important as they provide a guide to therapy

Optimal – treatment should be continued as the response predicts an excellent outcome and almost normal length of survival

Failure – treatment should be changed as the response predicts a significant risk of progression and death

Warning – intermediate category of response; ELN panel acknowledges that response and outcome could be better or inability to make a specific treatment recommendation on how to change treatment in order to improve response or outcome due to lack of solid data

FIRST-LINE TREATMENT

Any available or approved TKI

Imatinib 400 mg once daily

Nilotinib 300 mg twice daily

Dasatinib 100 mg once daily

ELN expert panel acknowledges that Nilotinib and Dasatinib are potent TKIs and induce a faster as well as deeper remission; however there is no solid evidence of better outcome

High-risk patients are eligible for clinical trials of 2nd generation TKIs. ELN expert panel believes that patients in AP or BP would benefit more after 2nd generation TKI therapy.

SECOND-LINE TREATMENT

ELN expert panel recommends change in therapy for

  • Failure/resistance
  • Side effects or toxic effects that prevent optimal dosing or affect QoL or threaten patient life

Any available TKI including Imatinib second-line after a 2nd generation TKI first-line if the change is due to side effects

Following sequence is recommended if the change is due to failure/resistance as Imatinib has no role

  • From Imatinib to Dasatinib, Nilotinib, Bosutinib or Ponatinib
  • From Nilotinib to Dasatinib, Bosutinib or Ponatinib
  • From Dasatinib to Nilotinib, Bosutinib or Ponatinib

Second-line treatment for patients with warning response is more complex and questionable due to lack of data demonstrating that change of medication would be useful

NCCN panel does not term response as warning; responses are considered to be either white (continue treatment) or black (change treatment)

THIRD-LINE TREATMENT

No evidence-based recommendations for patients who fail two or three TKIs

Causes for failure in this heterogeneous group of patients include low compliance, side effects and true cell resistance

Patients who fail 2 or 3 TKIs may require cell karyotype study, BCR-ABL1 kinase domain point mutation study and BM biopsy and stringent monitoring in preparation for SCT

Ponatinib may be efficient than any other TKI; however there are no comparative studies. Chemotherapy is considered to be more cosmetic than a useful treatment option.

MANAGEMENT OF CML, EVOLUTION OF GOALS AND STRATEGIES

Current ELN recommendations for CML management aim at achieving an MMR with a QoL and life duration as close to normal as possible

Chronic lifelong therapy is required in order to achieve and maintain goals by selecting among several TKIs

Current changing scenario is – goal moving from survival to cure and treatment policies moving towards discontinuation

Next goal – achieve treatment-free remission (TFR)

Goals are moving from survival to cure and treatment policies are moving towards discontinuation

It is not yet known how many patients would achieve the new goal of TFR and which medications/treatment policies are more likely to be successful

Extended use of more potent 2nd generation TKIs that induce faster and deeper molecular remissions would bring more patients into TFR

Early surrogate markers of TFR include decline in BCR-ABL1 transcripts, a BCR-ABL1 transcripts level ≤1 % within 3 months, ≤0.1 % within 1 year, and ≤0.01 % later on

OVERVIEW

Standard 1st line TKI therapy for newly diagnosed CP CML patients includes

  • Imatinib
  • Dasatinib
  • Nilotinib

Effective 2nd line therapy for patients resistant or intolerant to Imatinib include

  • Dasatinib
  • Nilotinib

Recently approved options for patients resistant or intolerant to prior TKI therapy include Bosutinib and Ponatinib

Early molecular response to 1st line TKI therapy is emerging as an effective prognostic indicator of long-term durable responses and survival

Molecular monitoring is necessary to identify patients who would benefit from alternate TKI therapy. QPCR is important for evaluation of treatment efficacy.

MONITORING MOLECULAR RESPONSE TO TKI THERAPY

The most sensitive assay available for measurement of BCR-ABL1 mRNA is QPCR

Strong correlation exists between QPCR results obtained from peripheral blood and bone marrow. Hence, it is not necessary to obtain BM aspirations.

QPCR to be performed prior to initiating TKI therapy

QPCR results are expressed as ratio of BCR-ABL1 transcript numbers to the number of control gene transcripts

QPCR results could also be expressed as a percentage

BCR-ABL1 mRNA transcripts can be detected after CCyR is achieved

Hence, QPCR remains the only tool capable of monitoring responses after patients have achieved CCyR

Equal copy numbers of BCR-ABL1 gene & control gene at the time of diagnosis is expressed as 100%. Choice of an appropriate control gene is important for generating reliable and reproducible data.

BCR, ABL, β-glucuronidase (GUSB), and β2 microglobulin (B2M) are the most widely studied for BCR-ABL1 quantification. IRIS trial used BCR as the control gene.

NCCN panel recommends QPCR (IS) with a sensitivity of ≥4.5 log reduction from baseline for molecular monitoring

In the absence of QPCR (IS), it is acceptable to use log-reduction from the laboratory-specific standardized baseline for monitoring molecular response

NCCN panel recommends QPCR (IS) monitoring every 3 months for all patients undergoing medical therapy including those who meet milestones at 3, 6, 12 and 18 months

After CCyR is achieved, QPCR (IS) monitoring is recommended every 3 months for 3 years and every 3-6 months thereafter

STANDARDIZATION USING THE INTERNATIONAL SCALE

National Institute of Health (NIH) Consensus Group proposed use of IS to standardize monitoring QPCR across laboratories

Use of 1 out of 3 genes – BCR, ABL, or GUSB + QPCR assay with sensitivity of at least 4 log reduction from standardized baseline is recommended

In IS, standardized baseline represents 100%

BCR-ABL1 transcript levels obtained in a given laboratory are converted to IS by applying a specific conversion factor (CF)

Standardized baseline is defined as median value of BCR-ABL1 mRNA at the time of diagnosis in 30 patients with CML as established in the IRIS study and it represents 100%

MMR, a 3-log reduction in BCR-ABL1 transcripts from standardized baseline is fixed at 0.1%

CCyR, a 2-log reduction in BCR-ABL1 transcripts from standardized baseline is fixed at 1%. MCyR, a 1-log reduction in BCR-ABL1 transcripts from standardized baseline is fixed at 10%.

CMR is defined as undetectable BCR-ABL1 transcripts with sensitivity of ≥4.5-log reduction from standardized baseline

For obtaining a laboratory-specific CF, every laboratory has to exchange 20-30 pre-treatment samples with a reference laboratory

Both laboratories analyse the samples and plot the results on a log scale for comparison

The anti-log of estimated mean bias between the methods is designated as the CF

After establishing the laboratory-specific CF, it is again validated through a second sample exchange with the reference laboratory

PROGNOSTIC SIGNIFICANCE OF EARLY MOLECULAR RESPONSE TO TKI THERAPY

Subset analysis of IRIS study revealed that incidence of disease progression was significantly high in patients who failed to achieve

  • 1-log reduction in BCR-ABL1 transcript levels by 3 months
  • 2-log reduction in BCR-ABL1 transcript levels by 6 months

Recent studies have showed that BCR-ABL1 transcript levels ≤10% after 3 months or ≤1% after 6 months of Imatininb therapy is an effective prognostic indicator for long-term outcomes

DASISION and ENESTnd, the 2 landmark studies have also demonstrated prognostic significance of EMR to 1st-line therapy with Dasatinib or Nilotinib among patients with newly-diagnosed CP CML

Quintas-Cardama et al report showed that

  • Patients with a BCR-ABL1/ABL1 transcript level >10% had a significantly lower probability of achieving a CCyR or MMR, and a higher probability of disease progression compared to those with BCR-ABL1 transcript levels of ≤10% at the same time point

DASISION study revealed that patients with a BCR-ABL1 transcript level of ≤10% (IS) at 3 months had significantly better 3-year PFS (93% vs 68% for dasatinib, P=0.0003; 96% vs 75% for imatinib, (P< 0.0001)

DASISION study also demonstrated that the rate of transformation was 3% (6 of 198 patients) for patients with BCR-ABL1 transcript levels of ≤10% at 3 months compared with 13.5% for those who did not reach this response milestone at 3 months

DASISION study revealed that the 3-year PFS was significantly better for patients with BCR-ABL1 transcript levels of ≤1% at 6 months (95% vs 85% for dasatinib, P=0020; 97% vs 84% for imatinib, P=0.0016)

DASISION study also demonstrated that the rate of transformation was 2% for patients with BCR-ABL1 transcript levels of ≤1% at 6 months compared with 9.7% for patients with BCR-ABL1 transcript levels >1%

ENESTnd study revealed that patients with BCR-ABL1 transcript levels of ≤10% at 3 months had significantly improved 4-year PFS compared to those with BCR-ABL1 transcript levels >10% at 3 months (95% vs 83% for nilotinib 300 mg, P=0.0061; 98% vs 83% for imatinib, P< 0.0001).

3-month molecular response after initiating 2nd-line TKI therapy is a predictor of OS and PFS among patients in CP CML after failure of Imatinib therapy

Recent studies have revealed that BCR-ABL1 transcript levels at 3 months provide a better prediction of long-term survival compared to levels at 6 months after 2nd-line TKI therapy among patients in CP CML

Milojkovic et al in their analysis of patients treated with Dasatinib or Nilotinib after Imatinib failure reported superior OS (91.3% vs 72.1%; P=0.02), and EFS (49.3% vs 13%; P< 0.001) rates for patients with a bcr-abl1 transcript level of ≤10% (is) at 3 months compared with those with a bcr-abl1 transcript level >10% (IS)

Branford et al also reported that molecular response at 3 months after second-line Nilotinib was predictive of EFS in patients with chronic-phase CML who are resistant or intolerant to Imatinib

It was also noticed that the estimated 24-month EFS rates were 82% and 48%, respectively, for patients with BCR-ABL1 transcript levels of ≤1% (IS) and those with BCR-ABL1 transcript levels >10% (IS) at 3 months after 2nd-line therapy with Nilotinib

SUMMARY

Availability of potent BCR-ABL1 TKIs has significantly improved outcomes of newly-diagnosed CML patients

Monitoring molecular response to TKI therapy with QPCR (IS) is more precise and less invasive

NCCN panel recommends regular molecular monitoring with QPCR (IS)

It is important to inform clinicians regarding significance of using QPCR standardized to IS in the clinical management of CML patients
